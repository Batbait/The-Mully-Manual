# Information Technology Manual

Information Technology was my gateway into the professional technology world.

While cybersecurity was ultimately my goal, I quickly learned that strong cybersecurity professionals rarely exist without a foundation in information technology.

Before working in IT, I spent four years as a Marine Corps Electro-Optical Ordnance Technician followed by several years working as an explosives operator for Northrop Grumman.

At first glance, those careers may seem completely unrelated to technology.

In reality, they taught many of the same skills that later helped me succeed in IT:

- Troubleshooting
- Documentation
- Attention to detail
- Following procedures
- Root cause analysis
- Working under pressure

My professional IT career began when I accepted a position as a Help Desk Technician at IBM while continuing my degree in Cybersecurity and Information Assurance.

That role introduced me to enterprise technology environments and taught me lessons that continue to influence the way I approach technical problems today.

Over time I progressed from Tier 1 responsibilities into Tier 2 support while continuing to expand my technical knowledge.

This manual documents the lessons I learned throughout that process.

## What Is Information Technology?

Information Technology is the practice of designing, maintaining, securing, and supporting the systems that allow organizations to function.

Examples include:

- Computers
- Servers
- Networks
- Applications
- Cloud platforms
- User accounts
- Mobile devices

Most people only notice technology when it stops working.

The purpose of IT is to keep it working.

## The Reality Of IT

One of the biggest misconceptions about IT is that the job revolves around fixing computers.

While troubleshooting is certainly part of the profession, IT is really about solving problems.

Often those problems involve:

- Technology
- Processes
- Communication
- Documentation
- Training

Sometimes the computer is perfectly fine.

The problem exists somewhere else entirely.

## Troubleshooting

If I had to identify the single most valuable skill in IT, it would be troubleshooting.

Every technical problem ultimately comes down to understanding:

- What is supposed to happen
- What is actually happening
- Why the difference exists

### A Simple Approach

When troubleshooting, I try to answer a few basic questions:

#### What Changed?

Many problems begin immediately after:

- Updates
- Configuration changes
- Hardware replacements
- User actions

Change is often a useful starting point.

#### Is The Problem Repeatable?

Can the issue be reproduced?

Consistent problems are usually easier to diagnose than intermittent ones.

#### Who Is Affected?

Determine whether the issue impacts:

- One user
- Multiple users
- One device
- Multiple devices
- An entire system

The scope of an issue often reveals useful clues.

#### What Does The Evidence Say?

Good troubleshooting relies on evidence, not assumptions.

Logs, error messages, screenshots, and testing are all valuable sources of information.

## The Help Desk

Many people view Help Desk as an entry-level position.

I view it as one of the best learning opportunities in technology.

Help Desk teaches:

- Customer service
- Communication
- Documentation
- Queue management
- Troubleshooting
- Time management

It also exposes you to a wide variety of technical problems.

No two days are exactly the same.

## Customer Service Matters

One lesson I learned quickly is that technical knowledge alone is not enough.

You also need to help people.

Most users don't care:

- Which service failed
- Which protocol broke
- Which server is down

They care about solving their problem.

Understanding how to communicate effectively is one of the most valuable skills a technology professional can develop.

## Documentation

Good documentation saves time.

Bad documentation creates problems.

Whenever possible, I document:

- Solutions
- Procedures
- Changes
- Troubleshooting steps

Future you will thank present you.

So will your coworkers.

## System Administration Fundamentals

Even if you are not a system administrator, understanding the basics is valuable.

Areas worth learning include:

### Windows Administration

Topics include:

- User accounts
- Active Directory
- Group Policy
- Permissions
- Windows troubleshooting

### Networking

Basic networking knowledge helps explain how systems communicate.

Key concepts include:

- IP addressing
- DNS
- DHCP
- Routing
- VPNs

### Cloud Services

Modern organizations increasingly rely on cloud platforms.

Common examples include:

- Microsoft 365
- Azure
- AWS
- Google Cloud

Even basic familiarity can be extremely beneficial.

## Career Development

One lesson I've learned is that careers are built gradually.

Many people focus entirely on their dream job.

I recommend focusing on the next job.

For me, that journey looked something like:

- Military service
- Manufacturing and defense work
- Help Desk Technician
- Tier 2 Support
- Continued cybersecurity development

Each step created opportunities for the next.

## Certifications

Certifications helped structure my learning and demonstrate initiative.

Some of the most commonly recommended certifications include:

- CompTIA A+
- Network+
- Security+
- CCNA

Certifications alone do not create expertise.

Experience is still essential.

However, certifications can provide valuable direction and help open doors.

## Lessons Learned

### Nobody Knows Everything

The longer I work in technology, the more obvious this becomes.

Even highly experienced professionals regularly encounter unfamiliar problems.

### Google Is A Skill

Knowing how to find reliable information quickly is incredibly valuable.

Most professionals spend far more time researching than many people realize.

### Stay Curious

Technology changes constantly.

A willingness to learn is often more important than existing knowledge.

### Communication Creates Opportunities

Some of the most successful people I've worked with weren't necessarily the most technical.

They were the ones who could explain technical concepts clearly and work effectively with others.

### Start Somewhere

Many people wait until they feel ready to enter technology.

The truth is that most of us start before we feel ready.

The important part is starting.

## Philosophy

Information Technology taught me that solving problems is ultimately more important than knowing answers.

Answers change.

Technology changes.

Products change.

The ability to analyze a problem, gather information, and work toward a solution remains valuable regardless of the platform involved.

Every ticket, every outage, every mistake, and every difficult problem presents an opportunity to learn.

The goal of this manual is not to present myself as an expert.

The goal is to document what I've learned so that someone else can begin their journey with a little more confidence than I had when I started mine.
