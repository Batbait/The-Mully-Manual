# Squirrel Hunting Manual

If deer hunting taught me patience and turkey hunting taught me communication, squirrel hunting taught me how to hunt.

Long before I worried about antlers, gobbles, or bear sign, I was learning how to move through the woods, spot movement, read habitat, and pay attention to details while pursuing squirrels throughout Appalachia.

In many ways, squirrel hunting is one of the best teachers a hunter can have.

Opportunities are frequent, mistakes are less costly, and the lessons learned apply to nearly every other form of hunting.

This manual documents the lessons I've learned hunting squirrels throughout the Appalachian Mountains.

## Why Hunt Squirrels?

Many hunters view squirrel hunting as a beginner's activity.

I think that's a mistake.

Squirrels can be surprisingly challenging and require many of the same skills used when pursuing larger game animals.

Squirrel hunting teaches:

- Woodsmanship
- Marksmanship
- Observation
- Patience
- Habitat recognition
- Shot discipline

The skills developed while hunting squirrels carry over into nearly every other style of hunting.

## Understanding Squirrels

Eastern gray squirrels are creatures of habit.

Much of their daily activity revolves around food, cover, and avoiding predators.

While they often appear carefree, squirrels spend much of their time watching and listening for danger.

### Daily Activity

Squirrels are usually most active:

- Early in the morning
- Late in the afternoon
- During calm weather

Activity often decreases during the hottest portions of the day.

### Seasonal Changes

Squirrel behavior changes dramatically throughout the year.

#### Early Season

Squirrels spend a great deal of time feeding.

Food sources are abundant and activity can be widespread.

#### Late Season

Leaves are gone and visibility improves.

Finding squirrels often becomes easier, but squirrels are also more cautious due to increased hunting pressure.

## Habitat

Throughout Appalachia, squirrels are commonly associated with:

- Oak forests
- Hickory forests
- Mixed hardwoods
- River bottoms
- Mature timber

Areas producing hard mast often hold the highest concentrations of squirrels.

### Food Sources

Some of the most productive locations I've found contain:

- Acorns
- Hickory nuts
- Beechnuts
- Walnuts

If food is present, squirrels usually are too.

## Scouting

Squirrel scouting is simple but effective.

Look for:

- Cut nutshells
- Feeding sign
- Active nests
- Bark stripped from trees

Fresh sign can quickly tell you whether squirrels are actively using an area.

## Hunting Strategies

### Sit And Listen

One of my favorite methods is simply finding a good location and sitting still.

After the woods settle down, squirrels often reveal themselves through:

- Moving branches
- Falling acorns
- Rustling leaves
- Bark chewing

Many times I've heard a squirrel long before I've seen it.

### Slow Walk Hunting

Another effective strategy is moving slowly through likely habitat.

The key word is slowly.

Most hunters move far too fast.

A squirrel that notices a rushed hunter often disappears before ever being seen.

### Focus On Trees

Many beginners spend too much time looking at the ground.

I try to focus on:

- Trunks
- Limbs
- Forks in trees
- Areas where branches cross

Squirrels often blend into their surroundings remarkably well.

## Equipment

Squirrel hunting doesn't require expensive equipment.

A simple setup is often the best.

### Common Gear

- .22 rifle
- Shotgun
- Comfortable boots
- Small day pack
- Water
- Blaze orange when required

The most important piece of equipment is still patience.

## Lessons Learned

### Move Slower

The single biggest lesson squirrels have taught me is to slow down.

Whenever I think I'm moving slowly enough, I can probably move slower.

### The Woods Need Time To Settle

Animals quickly return to normal behavior after disturbance.

Sometimes the best thing you can do is sit quietly and wait.

### Pay Attention To Sound

Many successful squirrel hunts begin with hearing something unusual.

A falling acorn.

A branch moving.

A faint rustle in the leaves.

The woods often tell you exactly where squirrels are if you're willing to listen.

## Philosophy

Squirrel hunting keeps me connected to the fundamentals of hunting.

There are no elaborate setups.

No complicated strategies.

No obsession with trophy animals.

Just a hunter, a patch of woods, and the challenge of paying attention.

Some of my favorite days outdoors have involved nothing more than wandering Appalachian hardwoods and chasing squirrels.

It's simple.

And that's exactly why I enjoy it.
