# Gear & Equipment Manual

One of the biggest mistakes new hunters make is believing success comes from buying more equipment.

It doesn't.

Good gear can make hunting easier, safer, and more comfortable, but no amount of expensive equipment can replace time spent in the woods.

This manual covers hunting gear I've used personally, equipment I've found valuable, and some advanced tools that may be worth considering for certain situations.

## Philosophy

When building a hunting kit, prioritize:

1. Safety
2. Reliability
3. Comfort
4. Effectiveness

In that order.

The most important gear is often the gear you forget you're carrying.

---

# Essential Safety Equipment

These are items I consider mandatory.

## Blaze Orange

If required by law, wear it.

Even when not required, additional visibility can be beneficial.

You cannot hunt if you've been mistaken for game.

## Compass

Technology fails.

Batteries die.

A basic compass weighs almost nothing and can save a miserable day from becoming a dangerous situation.

Learn how to use it before you need it.

## Physical Map

A printed map works regardless of:

- Cell service
- Battery life
- Weather

I strongly recommend carrying one in unfamiliar areas.

## Flashlight or Headlamp

Every hunter eventually stays out longer than expected.

Always carry a light.

Personally, I prefer headlamps because they leave both hands free.

### Spare Batteries

If your light requires batteries, carry extras.

## Footwear

Good boots are an investment.

You'll spend far more time standing, walking, climbing, and sitting in your footwear than using any weapon or gadget.

Comfort matters.

## Water

Dehydration makes everything worse.

Always carry more water than you think you'll need.

## First Aid Kit

Even a small kit is worthwhile.

Include:

- Bandages
- Antiseptic
- Pain relievers
- Any personal medications

# Basic Hunting Equipment

These items help turn a safe outing into a productive hunt.

## Weapon

The specifics depend on the season and regulations.

Examples include:

- Rifle
- Shotgun
- Muzzleloader
- Bow
- Crossbow

Use the equipment you are most proficient with.

Confidence matters.

## Hunting Knife

A good knife is one of the most useful tools you'll own.

Uses include:

- Field dressing
- Skinning
- General camp tasks

A sharp inexpensive knife is better than a dull expensive one.

## Binoculars

A quality set of binoculars can save countless miles of unnecessary walking.

Look for:

- Clear glass
- Good low-light performance
- Comfortable weight

## Binocular Harness

One of the best gear upgrades I've ever made.

A harness:

- Protects optics
- Reduces neck strain
- Keeps binoculars accessible

## Backpack

A day pack should comfortably carry:

- Water
- Snacks
- First aid
- Layers
- Game bags
- Navigation equipment

Avoid carrying unnecessary weight.

## Game Bags

Useful when:

- Packing out meat
- Quartering large game
- Protecting meat from dirt and insects

## Rope

A surprisingly versatile piece of equipment.

Uses include:

- Dragging game
- Hanging gear
- Securing loads

A small amount of rope can solve many problems.

## Gloves

Useful for:

- Cold weather
- Field dressing
- Handling rough materials

# Clothing Systems

Weather changes.

Your clothing should adapt with it.

## Base Layer

Designed to manage moisture.

Avoid cotton whenever possible.

## Insulating Layer

Provides warmth.

Examples include:

- Fleece
- Wool
- Synthetic insulation

## Outer Layer

Protects against:

- Wind
- Rain
- Vegetation

Adjust layers as conditions change.

# Advanced Equipment

Nice to have, but not necessary.

## Trail Cameras

One of the most useful scouting tools available.

Trail cameras help identify:

- Animal movement
- Travel corridors
- Activity times

Remember that trail cameras gather information.

They don't replace scouting.

## Rangefinder

Particularly useful for:

- Bowhunting
- Open-country hunting

Estimating distance accurately can greatly improve shot placement.

## Calls

Species-specific calls can increase opportunities.

Examples include:

- Turkey calls
- Deer grunt calls
- Predator calls

When used correctly, they can be extremely effective.

## Decoys

Most commonly used for:

- Turkey hunting
- Waterfowl hunting

Decoys can provide visual confirmation for approaching animals.

## Baits & Attractants

Where legal, baits and attractants can concentrate animal activity.

Examples include:

- Mineral sites
- Scent attractants
- Food-based attractants

Always verify local regulations before use.

## Scent Control Products

Opinions vary widely.

Some hunters swear by them.

Others don't.

I've found wind direction to be far more important than any scent-control product.

## Tree Stands & Saddles

Useful for:

- Deer hunting
- Improved visibility
- Reduced scent detection

Always use a safety harness.

# Technology

Modern technology has dramatically changed hunting.

## GPS Units

Advantages include:

- Reliable navigation
- Track recording
- Waypoint marking

Particularly valuable on large public lands.

## Smartphone Mapping Apps

Apps like:

- OnX
- HuntStand
- Gaia GPS

allow hunters to identify:

- Property lines
- Topography
- Access points
- Scouting locations

These have become some of the most valuable tools available to modern hunters.

## Trail Camera Networks

Cellular cameras allow remote monitoring without frequent visits.

Benefits include:

- Reduced disturbance
- Better scouting information

Drawback:

- Ongoing subscription costs

## Drones

In some circumstances, drones can be useful for:

- Mapping
- Property observation
- Habitat analysis

However, regulations vary significantly.

Always verify local laws before use.

Common equipment includes:

# Personal Preferences & Lessons Learned

The following opinions are based entirely on my own experiences hunting throughout Appalachia and coastal North Carolina.

Other hunters may feel differently, and that's perfectly fine. The purpose of this section is not to tell anyone what they should use, but to explain what has worked for me.

## Always Use A Sling

If I'm carrying a firearm, it has a sling.

No exceptions.

The only hunting weapon I regularly carry without a sling is a bow.

A good sling:

- Frees up your hands
- Reduces fatigue
- Improves safety while navigating obstacles
- Makes long hikes more comfortable

After spending enough time crossing streams, climbing ridges, crawling through brush, and dragging game, I have no interest in carrying a firearm by hand all day.

A sling is one of the most useful accessories you can add to a hunting firearm.

## The Turkey Vest Debate

Many hunters use backpacks.

I usually don't.

For most hunting situations, I prefer using a turkey vest.

The reasons are simple:

- Lightweight
- Compact
- Comfortable
- Easy access to gear

A good turkey vest allows me to carry:

- Calls
- Snacks
- Water
- Gloves
- Flashlights
- Maps
- Small tools

without the bulk of a traditional backpack.

I've also found turkey vests snag less frequently on brush than larger packs, particularly in thick Appalachian cover.

The seat cushion is an added bonus.

Even when I'm not hunting turkeys, I often find myself reaching for a turkey vest first.

## Rigid Boots Matter

One piece of equipment I refuse to compromise on is footwear.

Growing up and hunting in mountainous terrain taught me the importance of ankle support.

When traversing:

- Steep ridges
- Loose rock
- Deadfalls
- Creek crossings

I strongly prefer rigid boots with good ankle support.

While lightweight boots may be comfortable on flat ground, I've found stiffer boots provide more confidence and stability in difficult terrain.

Ankle injuries can end a hunt quickly.

Good boots help prevent them.

## Expensive Doesn't Mean Better

Over the years I've learned that expensive gear does not automatically result in success.

Some of my most memorable hunts involved:

- Simple equipment
- Older firearms
- Basic clothing
- Minimal technology

Meanwhile, I've seen hunters carrying thousands of dollars worth of gear who struggled because they lacked preparation or scouting.

Knowledge consistently outperforms equipment.

## Buy Once, Cry Once

While expensive gear isn't always necessary, I do believe certain items are worth spending money on.

Examples include:

- Boots
- Optics
- Rain gear
- Packs and vests
- Quality clothing

Cheap gear often becomes expensive gear after you've replaced it three times.

## The Best Gear Is The Gear You Actually Carry

The perfect piece of equipment does no good sitting at home.

I try to carry equipment that is:

- Practical
- Reliable
- Comfortable

If a piece of gear consistently gets left in the truck, it's probably not as important as I once thought.

## Final Thoughts

Every hunter eventually develops preferences.

Some are based on logic.

Some are based on experience.

Some are based on hard lessons learned in bad weather, rough terrain, or long walks back to the truck.

These preferences happen to be mine.

Your own experiences may lead you somewhere different, and that's part of becoming an outdoorsman.


## Philosophy

Good equipment makes hunting more enjoyable.

Good judgment makes hunting successful.

When forced to choose between expensive gear and additional time spent scouting, practicing, or learning, choose the experience every time.

The equipment will eventually wear out.

The knowledge stays with you forever.
