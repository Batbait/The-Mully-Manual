# Bow Fishing Manual

Bowfishing is one of the most unique outdoor activities I've ever participated in.

Unlike traditional fishing, bowfishing combines elements of hunting and fishing into a highly active experience. Instead of waiting for a bite, you're constantly scanning the water, looking for opportunities, judging distance, and trying to place an arrow where a fish actually is rather than where it appears to be.

I am by no means a bowfishing expert. In fact, I've only been on three dedicated bowfishing trips throughout my life. However, each trip taught me something new and gave me a greater appreciation for both the sport and the environments where it takes place.

This manual documents those experiences and the lessons I learned from them.

## My Experiences

### New River, North Carolina

My first real exposure to bowfishing took place on the New River near Jacksonville, North Carolina.

These waters are heavily influenced by the tide and range from fresh to brackish depending on location and conditions. We targeted spotted gar and longnose gar, both of which are well-suited for bowfishing due to their habit of cruising near the surface.

The experience taught me how difficult it can be to spot fish in unfamiliar water, especially when navigating tide-driven systems far different from the mountain rivers I grew up fishing.

### Camp Lejeune Coast

Another memorable trip took place closer to the coast near Camp Lejeune.

Instead of targeting fish, we pursued stingrays at night in shallow coastal waters.

Watching large rays appear beneath the lights was a completely different experience from anything I had encountered in freshwater. It reinforced how important visibility, teamwork, and good communication are when bowfishing from a boat.

### Potomac River Near Quantico

My most productive bowfishing experience was on the Potomac River near Quantico, Virginia.

There we targeted carp and catfish while working larger, deeper waterways than what I was accustomed to back home.

The Potomac provided ideal conditions for boat-based bowfishing. Compared to the upper Potomac near my home, the water was generally deeper, more navigable, and better suited to running specialized bowfishing boats.

Many of the waters I fish today are simply too shallow or rocky for that style of bowfishing to be practical.

## Why Night Bowfishing?

Every bowfishing trip I've participated in has taken place after dark.

Strong lights mounted to the boat illuminate the water, making fish visible while reducing the amount of pressure they experience from anglers and boat traffic during the day.

Nighttime also provides:

- Cooler temperatures
- Less recreational boat traffic
- Better visibility under lights
- More active fish in many situations

For me, bowfishing and nighttime have always gone hand in hand.

## Equipment

### Bow

Most bowfishers use either:

- Recurve bows
- Older compound bows
- Purpose-built bowfishing bows

Reliability is generally more important than speed.

### Reel

Bowfishing reels are designed to retrieve line attached directly to the arrow.

Common styles include:

- Bottle reels
- Retriever reels
- Spincast-style reels

### Arrows

Bowfishing arrows are significantly more durable than standard hunting arrows and are designed to withstand impact with fish, rocks, and submerged obstacles.

### Lights

For nighttime bowfishing, lighting is everything.

Without sufficient lighting, spotting fish becomes extremely difficult.

Most dedicated boats use powerful LED lighting systems to illuminate the water ahead of the boat.

## Lessons Learned

### Fish Appear Higher Than They Really Are

The first lesson every bowfisher learns is that water bends light.

Fish are always deeper than they appear.

When in doubt:

**Aim lower than your instincts tell you to.**

### Slow Down

Most beginners focus on taking shots.

The real challenge is learning to identify fish in the first place.

Developing your eyes is often more important than improving your shooting.

### Every Environment Is Different

One of the things I enjoy most about bowfishing is how different every trip feels.

Brackish rivers, coastal flats, and large tidal rivers all present unique challenges.

The techniques that work in one environment may not work in another.

## Philosophy

Bowfishing gave me a new perspective on both hunting and fishing.

It taught me to pay closer attention to water, fish behavior, and environmental conditions. It also reminded me how much there is still to learn every time I step into a new ecosystem.

While I don't bowfish nearly as often as I traditional fish, every trip has been memorable, educational, and worth the effort.

That's ultimately what The Mully Manual is about:

not pretending to know everything, but documenting experiences honestly so that the lessons aren't forgotten.
