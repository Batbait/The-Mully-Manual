# Hunting Manual

Hunting has been one of the defining parts of my life for as long as I can remember.

I grew up in the Appalachian Mountains along the North Branch of the Potomac River near Ridgeley, West Virginia. From an early age, I was fortunate enough to learn from experienced hunters who taught me not only how to pursue game, but also how to respect the land, wildlife, and traditions that make hunting worthwhile.

Over the years I've hunted whitetail deer, turkey, black bear, squirrel, and many other species throughout Appalachia. Through success, failure, and countless hours spent in the woods, I've learned that hunting is about much more than filling tags. It is about understanding the natural world and developing the patience to become part of it.

My time in the Marine Corps broadened my hunting experience beyond the mountains I grew up in. While stationed at Camp Lejeune, North Carolina, I had to adapt to entirely different environments. Thick swamps, coastal wetlands, pine forests, and flat terrain presented challenges that were completely different from the ridges, hollows, and hardwood forests of Appalachia.

Every new environment taught me something valuable. Animal behavior changes. Terrain changes. Weather changes. The fundamentals remain the same, but the application is always evolving.

This manual is my effort to document the lessons, experiences, successes, failures, and observations I've collected along the way.

I do not claim to be an expert. I'm simply someone who loves spending time in the woods and believes that every season has something to teach.

## Philosophy

The best hunters are not necessarily the most successful.

They are the ones who continue learning.

Every empty stand, missed opportunity, blown stalk, and unsuccessful season carries a lesson. Often those lessons teach more than success ever will.
