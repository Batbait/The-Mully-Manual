# Cybersecurity Manual

Cybersecurity is the field that ultimately convinced me to pursue a career in technology.

Unlike many people who enter cybersecurity through computer science, networking, or software development, my path was somewhat unconventional. I began my professional career as a Marine Corps Electro-Optical Ordnance Technician before later working as an explosives operator for Northrop Grumman.

While I had always been interested in technology, two events convinced me that cybersecurity was the career I wanted to pursue.

The first was witnessing my mother's success working in the information technology field. Growing up around technology gave me an appreciation for both the opportunities and challenges that came with the industry. It also showed me that a career in technology could provide stability, growth, and the chance to continually learn.

The second was the Colonial Pipeline cyberattack in 2021. Watching a cybersecurity incident disrupt fuel distribution across a large portion of the United States made me realize how dependent modern society is on secure systems. More importantly, it demonstrated that cybersecurity failures don't just affect computers. They affect businesses, infrastructure, and everyday people trying to live their lives.

Seeing both the opportunities that technology could provide and the consequences of poor cybersecurity motivated me to pursue the field seriously.

What began as curiosity soon became a commitment. I started pursuing industry certifications, enrolled in Western Governors University's Cybersecurity and Information Assurance program, and eventually transitioned into the information technology industry while completing my degree.

Cybersecurity is simply too broad and changes too quickly for anyone to know everything.

My goal is to document what I've learned so that others can start their journey with a better roadmap than I had.

## What Is Cybersecurity?

At its core, cybersecurity is the protection of:

- Information
- Systems
- Networks
- Applications
- Devices
- Users

from threats that could compromise confidentiality, integrity, or availability.

Despite what movies often portray, cybersecurity is rarely about hacking.

More often, it is about:

- Managing risk
- Protecting assets
- Reducing vulnerabilities
- Detecting threats
- Recovering from incidents

Cybersecurity is ultimately a business function.

The goal is not perfect security.

The goal is to reduce risk to an acceptable level.

## Security Fundamentals

Every security concept eventually traces back to a few foundational principles.

### Confidentiality

Information should only be accessible to authorized individuals.

Examples include:

- Encryption
- Access controls
- Permissions
- Authentication

### Integrity

Information should remain accurate and trustworthy.

Examples include:

- Hashing
- File validation
- Digital signatures
- Change management

### Availability

Systems and data must remain accessible when needed.

Examples include:

- Backups
- Redundancy
- Disaster recovery planning
- High-availability systems

Together, these three principles form what is commonly known as the CIA Triad.

Everything in cybersecurity ultimately supports one or more of these objectives.

## Security Principles

### Least Privilege

Users should only have the permissions necessary to perform their duties.

No more.

No less.

### Defense In Depth

No single security control should be trusted as the sole line of defense.

Security works best when multiple layers are present.

Examples include:

- Firewalls
- Anti-malware
- MFA
- Application controls
- Security awareness training

### Zero Trust

Trust should never be automatically granted simply because a user or device is inside a network.

Verification should occur continuously.

### Assume Breach

Modern organizations increasingly operate with the assumption that an attacker may eventually gain access.

Security planning focuses on:

- Detection
- Containment
- Response
- Recovery

rather than prevention alone.

## Common Threats

Some of the most common threats organizations face include:

### Phishing

Attempts to trick users into revealing information or performing actions.

### Malware

Malicious software designed to:

- Steal information
- Encrypt data
- Disrupt systems
- Establish persistence

### Social Engineering

Manipulating people rather than technology.

Often the easiest way for an attacker to gain access.

### Insider Threats

Not every threat originates outside an organization.

Employees, contractors, and trusted users can create significant risk, whether intentionally or accidentally.

## Security Best Practices

### Use Multi-Factor Authentication

MFA remains one of the most effective defenses available to individuals and organizations.

### Keep Systems Updated

Patching vulnerabilities reduces attack surfaces and limits opportunities for exploitation.

### Use Strong Passwords

Password reuse remains one of the easiest ways to compromise accounts.

Use unique passwords whenever possible.

### Back Up Important Data

Even the best security controls cannot guarantee prevention.

Recovery planning is essential.

### Think Before Trusting

Many attacks succeed because users act before thinking.

Take time to verify unexpected requests.

## Breaking Into Cybersecurity

One of the biggest misconceptions I encountered is the belief that cybersecurity is an entry-level field.

For most people, it isn't.

The strongest cybersecurity professionals often build foundations in:

- Help Desk
- System Administration
- Networking
- Cloud
- Software Development

before transitioning into security roles.

My own path started in IT support.

That experience taught me:

- Troubleshooting
- Documentation
- Enterprise systems
- User support
- Problem-solving

All of which remain valuable in security.

## Certifications

Certifications played a major role in my transition into technology.

While certifications alone do not guarantee employment, they helped structure my learning and demonstrate commitment to potential employers.

### Entry-Level Certifications

Examples include:

- CompTIA A+
- CompTIA Network+
- CompTIA Security+

These provide foundational knowledge and are often excellent starting points.

### Intermediate Certifications

Examples include:

- CySA+
- PenTest+
- CCNA
- CASP+

These build upon existing technical experience and specialization.

### Advanced Certifications

Examples include:

- CISSP
- CISM
- GIAC Certifications

These generally become more valuable after gaining professional experience.

## Lessons Learned

### Nobody Starts Ready

Every cybersecurity professional starts somewhere.

The goal is progress, not perfection.

### Focus On Fundamentals

Tools change.

Fundamentals endure.

A strong understanding of:

- Networking
- Operating systems
- Authentication
- Risk management

will outlast any particular technology trend.

### Communication Matters

Some of the most valuable cybersecurity professionals are not the most technical.

They are the ones who can explain technical concepts clearly.

### Curiosity Is A Competitive Advantage

Technology changes constantly.

The people who remain curious tend to remain valuable.

### Experience Matters

Certifications and degrees can open doors.

Experience keeps them open.

## Advice For Career Changers

As someone who entered technology after military service and careers in unrelated fields, my advice is straightforward:

Start before you feel ready.

You will never know everything.

No one does.

Find opportunities to learn.

Build practical skills.

Ask questions.

Accept mistakes.

Remain curious.

Progress comes from consistency, not perfection.

## Philosophy

Cybersecurity is not about becoming the smartest person in the room.

It is about continually learning, adapting, and solving problems.

Every new threat creates an opportunity to learn.

Every challenge reveals a knowledge gap worth exploring.

The goal of this manual is not to present myself as an authority.

The goal is to document the lessons I've learned so others can begin their own journey with a little more direction than I had when I started.
