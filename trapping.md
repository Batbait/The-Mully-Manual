# Trapping Manual

Trapping taught me to pay attention to details that most outdoorsmen overlook.

Unlike hunting or fishing, trapping continues to work long after you've gone home. Success depends less on perfect timing and more on preparation, location, understanding animal behavior, and learning how wildlife moves through the landscape.

Over the years I've trapped a variety of animals, though raccoons make up the majority of my experience.

Species I have personally trapped include:

- Raccoons
- Opossums
- Fishers
- Foxes
- Coyotes

I've also pursued beavers and muskrats, though I've yet to successfully connect with either species.

During my time in North Carolina, I volunteered to be included on a list of trappers available to assist with nuisance wildlife issues. While I only received one call, the experience provided an interesting perspective on the role trapping plays beyond recreation and fur harvesting.

This manual documents the lessons I've learned through successes, failures, and many mornings spent checking trap lines.

## Why Trap?

Many people misunderstand trapping.

To some, it seems old-fashioned.

To others, it appears mysterious.

In reality, trapping remains an important wildlife management tool.

Trapping teaches:

- Animal behavior
- Reading sign
- Patience
- Problem solving
- Habitat recognition
- Attention to detail

A successful trapper learns to think like the animal they're pursuing.

## Understanding Animals

One of the first lessons trapping teaches is that animals are creatures of habit.

They tend to:

- Use the same travel routes
- Visit the same food sources
- Follow predictable terrain features
- Seek cover in similar locations

The trap itself is often less important than understanding where an animal already wants to go.

## Reading Sign

Good trappers spend a great deal of time looking for evidence.

### Tracks

Tracks provide valuable information about:

- Species
- Direction of travel
- Activity levels
- Size

Fresh tracks often reveal where an animal has been within the last few hours.

### Trails

Many species consistently use established travel routes.

Look for:

- Worn paths
- Fence crossings
- Creek crossings
- Gaps in vegetation

These locations often present excellent trapping opportunities.

### Feeding Sign

Animals leave evidence wherever they feed.

Signs may include:

- Disturbed soil
- Shelled food
- Scat
- Damaged vegetation

Food sources naturally attract wildlife.

## Trap Types

One of the first things a new trapper learns is that there is no universal trap.

Different species, environments, regulations, and objectives call for different equipment.

Selecting the correct trap is often just as important as selecting the correct location.

### Foothold Traps

Foothold traps are among the most versatile traps available.

When used properly, they restrain an animal by the foot until the trapper arrives.

Advantages include:

- Extremely versatile
- Suitable for many species
- Effective in a wide variety of habitats
- Easy to conceal

Common target species include:

- Fox
- Coyote
- Raccoon
- Fisher
- Bobcat
- Beaver

### Offset Jaw Traps

Many modern foothold traps utilize offset jaws.

Offset jaws leave a slight gap between the jaws when closed.

Advantages may include:

- Reduced injury potential
- Improved holding ability
- Improved circulation

Many trappers prefer offset jaws when targeting larger furbearers such as coyotes and foxes.

### Bodygrip Traps (Conibears)

Bodygrip traps are designed to dispatch an animal quickly.

These traps are particularly popular for:

- Beaver
- Muskrat
- Raccoon

Common locations include:

- Water channels
- Culverts
- Runs
- Den entrances

Placement is critical because bodygrip traps depend heavily on forcing an animal through a predictable path.

### Cage Traps (Live Traps)

Commonly referred to by many hunters as "Have-A-Hart" traps, live cage traps capture animals without immediately dispatching them.

Common targets include:

- Raccoons
- Opossums
- Skunks
- Feral cats
- Nuisance wildlife

Advantages:

- Live capture
- Easy to use
- Selective

Disadvantages:

- Large
- Heavy
- Less effective for many wary predators

Before relocating wildlife, always verify your local regulations.

## Raccoons

Most of my trapping experience comes from targeting raccoons.

Raccoons are intelligent, adaptable, and widespread throughout much of North America.

### Habitat

Look around:

- Creek bottoms
- Riverbanks
- Agricultural areas
- Hardwoods
- Wetlands

### Characteristics

Raccoons are curious animals.

That curiosity frequently works against them when traps are properly placed.

Because raccoons are abundant and adaptable, they can be an excellent species for new trappers to learn from.

## Opossums

Opossums often show up where raccoons are found.

They are opportunistic feeders and commonly travel:

- Creek corridors
- Wooded edges
- Agricultural areas

While not always the primary target, they are a regular part of many trap lines.

## Foxes

Foxes tend to be more cautious than raccoons.

Success often depends on:

- Careful set placement
- Understanding travel patterns
- Paying attention to wind and scent

Foxes taught me that small details matter.

## Coyotes

Coyotes are among the most challenging species I've pursued.

They are intelligent, adaptable, and highly aware of changes in their environment.

Many trapping mistakes that a raccoon might ignore will immediately alert a coyote.

Because of this, coyotes demand attention to detail and patience.
