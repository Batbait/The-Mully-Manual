# Fishing Field Notes

This section of The Mully Manual serves as both a fishing journal and an informal record of the North Branch Potomac River and nearby waters.

My goal is to document:

- Water conditions
- Fish populations
- Seasonal patterns
- Successful lures and presentations
- River health observations
- Weather impacts
- Personal lessons learned

Over time, I hope these entries develop into a useful record of both my growth as an angler and the changes occurring within my local waterways.

## About The River

I grew up along the North Branch of the Potomac River near Ridgeley, West Virginia.

I've been fishing these waters for as long as I can remember.

The North Branch has experienced dramatic changes over the decades. For much of the twentieth century, pollution from coal mining, acid mine drainage, industrial activity, and wastewater severely damaged aquatic life throughout portions of the watershed. In some areas, sections of the river were considered biologically dead. Recovery efforts beginning in the 1990s greatly improved water quality and allowed fish populations to rebound. Today, many stretches support healthy recreational fisheries that would have been impossible during the river's worst years. 

While I am not a fisheries biologist, I have witnessed noticeable changes during my lifetime.

As a child, I remember catching impressive bluegill, abundant sunfish, bass, catfish, and the occasional walleye. Fishing seemed excellent.

Then there was a period, particularly during my teenage and early adult years, where my perception was that fishing became significantly more difficult.

Whether that decline was caused by environmental conditions, angling pressure, changes in my own fishing habits, or simple selective memory is difficult to know.

After returning home in 2022 following military service, I have felt that fishing success has improved once again. Fish seem more plentiful than I remember during the previous decade, though these observations are entirely anecdotal and may be influenced by increased experience and time spent on the water.

This section exists partly to replace memories with data.

Rather than relying solely on recollection, I intend to document observations consistently and allow long-term patterns to reveal themselves.

## Recent Observations

One event that stands out in recent memory was a major sewage-related incident upstream of my home that temporarily impacted recreational use of portions of the river.

For a significant period of time, many people understandably avoided using affected sections of the river. Fewer anglers, paddlers, swimmers, and recreational users were present than I had become accustomed to seeing.

After returning home and spending more time on the water, I began noticing what felt like improved fishing compared to what I remembered from my teens and early twenties. Whether this perception is the result of reduced fishing pressure, natural population fluctuations, improvements in water quality, increased personal experience, selective memory, or some combination of those factors is impossible for me to say with certainty.

Disclaimer

These field notes are not scientific studies.

They are personal observations recorded by an angler who has spent much of his life on and around the North Branch Potomac River.

Whenever possible, I will clearly distinguish between observed facts, personal opinions, and assumptions.
One of the goals of these field notes is to separate observation from assumption.

By recording conditions, catches, species, and patterns over time, I hope to build a more objective picture of what is happening in the river rather than relying solely on memory.

## Observations

Second day fly fishing. Brought my partner Lily with me, she was using an ultralight spinning rod with braided line and a crayfish pattern crankbait. I was using my 8/9wt fly rod with a black woolly bugger. 

Began feeling more comfortable with streamer presentations and fly casting. Successfully landed my first fish on a fly rod, a rock bass. Caught it by drifting line along submerged root ball and streaming it past the structure rapidly. 

Still struggling with fly selection and understanding nymphs and dry flies, but streamers are beginning to make sense.

## Lessons Learned

The fish care far less about perfect casting than I do.

Getting the fly in productive water matters more than making a beautiful cast.
