# Fly Fishing Manual

Unlike many of the topics covered in The Mully Manual, fly fishing is not something I've spent my entire life doing.

As of writing this, I've only been fly fishing twice.

That may not sound like much, but every skill starts somewhere, and one of the goals of The Mully Manual is to document the learning process honestly. Rather than pretending to be an expert, this manual will serve as a record of what I learn as I progress.

So far, I've spent two days on the water with a fly rod and managed to catch my first fish on the fly: a rock bass from my local river.

More importantly, I finally started to understand how a streamer is meant to be cast and fished.

## How I Got Started

Like many beginners, I made my first mistake before I ever touched the water.

I purchased an 8/9 weight fly rod combo before learning much about fly fishing.

Unfortunately, most fish in my local waters are about the size of my hand.

The result is that I'm currently using a setup better suited for much larger fish than the species I'm commonly targeting.

It's funny now, but it was also an important lesson:

Research first. Buy gear second.

That said, the outfit works, and I'd rather spend time learning to fish than worrying about having the perfect equipment.

## What I've Learned So Far

### Fly Casting Is Different Than Conventional Fishing

My first realization was how different fly casting is from everything else.

With spinning and baitcasting equipment, the lure provides the weight that carries the line.

With fly fishing, the line itself carries the fly.

That sounds obvious now, but understanding that concept helped everything start making more sense.

### Slow Down

When I first started, I wanted to cast too hard and too fast.

The fly rod works better when you allow it to load and do the work for you.

Once I relaxed and focused on timing rather than power, my casts immediately improved.

### Streamers Feel Familiar

Of the fly fishing techniques I've tried so far, streamers make the most sense to me.

As someone with a background in conventional fishing, fishing a streamer feels somewhat similar to working a lure through the water.

While I still have a great deal to learn, streamers are currently where I feel most comfortable.

## Things I Still Need To Learn

At the moment, there are several areas where I have very little experience.

### Dry Flies

I understand the basic concept of imitating insects floating on the surface, but I haven't yet developed confidence fishing them.

### Nymphing

This remains somewhat of a mystery to me.

Many experienced fly anglers consider nymphing one of the most effective methods available, but I have yet to dedicate enough time to learn it properly.

### Fly Selection

Right now, choosing flies feels a little overwhelming.

I know enough to know I don't know enough.

This is an area I'll continue updating as I gain experience.

## Future Experiments

One thing I'm particularly interested in is fishing crayfish patterns.

Growing up on Appalachian rivers taught me that crayfish are an extremely important food source for many species, especially smallmouth bass.

My instincts tell me that a well-presented crayfish imitation should perform exceptionally well in my local waters.

Whether that's actually true remains to be tested.

## Lessons Learned

The biggest lesson fly fishing has taught me so far is that being a beginner is okay.

It's easy to become frustrated when learning a new skill, especially after spending years becoming comfortable and confident using conventional fishing gear.

But that's also what makes learning rewarding.

Every improvement is noticeable.

Every fish is meaningful.

Every outing teaches something new.

## Philosophy

This manual is different from many others on this site.

It isn't a collection of lifetime knowledge.

It's a record of knowledge being built.

Years from now, I expect this section to look very different. Hopefully I'll laugh at some of the things I believed as a beginner.

Until then, this manual will document the journey exactly as it happens: one cast, one lesson, and one fish at a time.
